I'm a technology professional based in Metro Vancouver with a few decades of accumulated scar tissue from working across telecom systems, government infrastructure, retro computing, and everything in between. I've spent most of my career finding unexpected ways to connect existing systems — absorbing complexity so other people don't have to.

## The Early Years

My first serious act of programming hubris was writing a **compiler for a finite state machine language** in the telecom industry. Not using a compiler — writing one. This set a tone.

From there I spent time configuring **X.25 network nodes**, which means I have opinions about packet-switched networking that nobody asks for anymore. I also automated government database workflows at a time when "automation" mostly meant convincing legacy systems to talk to each other without anyone noticing.

## The Middle Period (Things Get Weirder)

I built `pgate`, a tool for propagating commands in parallel across heterogeneous machine fleets — because sometimes you need to do a thing to *all the servers* and you'd rather not do it one at a time while quietly losing your mind.

I integrated **Perforce with ClearQuest**, which is either impressive or a cry for help depending on your perspective.

I maintained a **VAX/VMS simulation environment using SimH**, because some systems deserve to keep running long after the hardware has turned to dust, and also because VMS has a certain austere dignity that modern operating systems lack.

## The Creative Detours

Alongside the infrastructure work, there's always been a parallel track of projects that exist somewhere between engineering and obsession:

- **CWP!** — a reconstructed Apple //e BASIC program, because the Apple II deserves respect
- **Bidet** — a Sixel large-font tool (the name is funnier in context)
- **"Name in Lights"** — a marquee implementation spanning VMS, Apple II, DOS, and Perl/Windows, because one platform was never enough
- **Adventures in Terminal Land** — a [YouTube channel](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W0wiSz2KoqA&list=PLeUBBmcqYU_8Q2FG3QUi-O98GCkm4vNwF&pp=sAgC) documenting retro computing projects for the small but enthusiastic audience that finds this sort of thing as compelling as I do

More recently: a **haunted/possessed LCD clock** with a Snake easter egg, a paranoia engine, segment drift animation, and synthesized haunted sounds. Also **Maze4**, a fruitbot racing game with procedural sound effects and live commentary. These exist because they should.

## The Present

I work as an **Employment Facilitator at WorkBC**, helping people find sustainable work through direction, encouragement, and practical tools. It turns out that absorbing complexity so others don't have to is a transferable skill.

I'm also a **professional Laughter Yoga leader** with nearly 20 years of experience, operating under [universal-laughter.com](https://universal-laughter.com). If you're wondering how the same person ends up writing FSM compilers *and* leading laughter yoga sessions, so am I, but here we are.

I cycle to work. I have deep conversations. I find the whole situation — civilizational, technological, existential — genuinely interesting, even when it's alarming.

## The Philosophy

My approach to technical problems has always been the same: look at what already exists, figure out what it's capable of that nobody's used yet, and connect the things. Straightforward over clever. Working over elegant. Durable over fashionable.

Also: if you can make it a little bit haunted, make it a little bit haunted.
